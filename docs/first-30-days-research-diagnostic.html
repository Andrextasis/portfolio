First 30 Days Research Diagnostic

How I would enter a product, service, or people-insights team and turn unclear behavior into useful decisions.

1. Clarify the Decision

I would start by separating the research question from the business or team decision. A useful study is not just "understand users"; it should make a next action clearer. Examples: improve onboarding, reduce confusion, validate a daily-use feature, understand dropout, or identify where a support process creates anxiety.

  • What decision will this research change?
  • Who needs confidence: recruiter, product lead, support, education, or people team?
  • What evidence already exists and what is still assumed?

2. Map the Behavioral Context

My psychology background helps me look beyond surface preferences. I would map motivation, comprehension, trust, social pressure, emotional load, and repeated-use friction. In sensitive contexts, I would also identify what should not be collected publicly or casually.

  • Observed behavior vs. stated preference.
  • Moments of confusion, avoidance, uncertainty, or fatigue.
  • Language level, context, device constraints, and support needs.

3. Choose Lightweight Methods

I would keep the method proportional to the decision. For early teams, a clear small study is often more valuable than a perfect large one. Likely methods include stakeholder interviews, user interviews, simple usability tasks, survey cleanup, support-ticket review, competitor scan, or a structured desk review.

  • 5 to 8 focused user interviews when the question is motivational or contextual.
  • Usability tasks when the question is interface friction.
  • Survey or form audit when the question is intake quality.
  • Internal workflow review when the question is service or people operations.

4. Synthesize for Action

The output should be useful to a decision-maker. I would separate observations, interpretations, risks, and recommendations. I would avoid overclaiming and label confidence clearly.

  • Top user needs and friction points.
  • Evidence table with source, signal strength, and limitation.
  • Decision options with tradeoffs.
  • Next test or product change.

5. What I Would Show in an Interview

I can walk through a sanitized example using Fuel, Weight, Deskpad, or a hypothetical service flow. I would show how I define the problem, choose methods, protect sensitive information, and turn findings into a next product or people decision.
